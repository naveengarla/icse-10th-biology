# Chapter 2 — Deep Conceptual Practice Questions 🧠

**Structure of Chromosomes, Cell Cycle and Cell Division**

> **57 questions** organised by chapter section — read a section, then practise it immediately.  
> Work through questions **slowly** — visualise each process before answering.  
> Difficulty ranges from ICSE board-level to IIT-style multi-step reasoning.

## Section 2.1 — What Are Chromosomes?

**Q1.** Why does the same genetic material exist in two physically different forms — chromatin fibres versus condensed chromosomes — inside the same nucleus at different times? What problem does each form solve?

**Q2.** In interphase, the nucleus appears "empty" or fuzzy under a light microscope, yet it is the most active phase of the cell cycle. Explain this apparent contradiction using the packaging logic of chromatin.

**Q3.** The word "chromosome" comes from the Greek *chroma* (colour) and *soma* (body). At which stage of the cell cycle would chromosomes be most visible under staining, and why do they become invisible as distinct structures during interphase — even though the genetic material is still fully present?

**Q4.** *(Error Detection)* A student claims: *"Interphase is a resting phase because chromosomes are not visible."* Identify the misconception and explain what is actually happening at the molecular level during this phase.

## Section 2.2 — Discovery of Chromosomes

**Q5.** Walther Fleming discovered chromosomes in 1882 by studying rapidly dividing cells of salamander larvae rather than adult non-dividing cells. Why would rapidly dividing cells be a better specimen for initially observing chromosomes under a light microscope?

**Q6.** Fleming named the process he observed "mitosis" from the Greek word for "thread." What was the "thread" he was referring to, and at which specific stage of mitosis would this threadlike appearance be most visible to a 19th-century light microscopist?

**Q7.** Fleming's older microscope showed him "minute threads appearing to divide lengthwise." We now know this was chromatid separation at the centromere. Which specific stage of mitosis was he most likely observing, and what would a stained slide at that stage look like?

## Section 2.3 — Structure of Chromosomes & DNA

**Q8.** A replicated chromosome looks like an "X". What exact molecular event creates this shape, and why does the cell deliberately keep the two halves attached until a specific moment?

**Q9.** Imagine a single human chromosome containing roughly a million nucleosomes. What does this number reveal about the scale of compaction the cell must achieve, and why is stepwise (not single-step) packing essential?

**Q10.** The centromere is often called the "waist" or "handle" of the chromosome. Why is its precise location on every chromosome non-negotiable for accurate cell division?

**Q11.** If you were to draw the hierarchy **Cell → Nucleus → Chromosome → Chromatin fibre → Nucleosome → DNA double helix → Gene** on a single page, what single principle connects every level?

**Q12.** During S-phase, DNA content doubles, yet the chromosome number remains the same. How is this possible? What does it tell you about the relationship between a chromatid and a chromosome?

**Q13.** Why does the cell invest enormous energy in super-coiling chromatin into chromosomes just before division, only to de-condense them immediately after? Walk through the mechanical problems that would arise without this switch.

**Q14.** Suppose a mutation prevents histone proteins from forming nucleosomes. Predict, step by step, what would happen to a 2-metre-long DNA molecule inside a 6-micrometre nucleus during attempted cell division.

**Q15.** Adenine always pairs with Thymine (connected by 2 hydrogen bonds) and Guanine always pairs with Cytosine (connected by 3 hydrogen bonds). If one DNA strand reads A–T–C–G, write the complementary strand. Explain why this strict base-pairing rule is what makes faithful DNA replication possible.

**Q16.** What if, during S-phase, DNA replication occurred but the new strands were not complementary (wrong base pairing throughout)? How would this affect the next generation of cells?

**Q17.** *(Error Detection)* A diagram labels the structure formed by DNA wrapped around a core of eight histone molecules as a "chromosome." What is wrong with this labelling? What is the correct hierarchical name for this structure?

**Q18.** Link the nucleosome packaging of DNA with the need for DNA replication in S-phase. Why must the entire packaging hierarchy be disassembled and reassembled every cell cycle?

**Q19.** A researcher observes that in a mutant cell line, nucleosomes form normally but super-coiling fails. Construct a step-by-step causal chain explaining why these cells would show high rates of chromosome breakage specifically during anaphase.

## Section 2.4 — What Are Genes?

**Q20.** Genes are defined as "specific sequences of nucleotides on a chromosome that encode particular proteins which express as a feature of the body." Trace this chain — **gene → protein → visible feature** — using a concrete human example such as eye colour or blood group.

**Q21.** DNA fingerprinting relies on the non-coding regions between genes. Using the structure of chromosomes, explain why these non-coding regions show high individual variation while the genes themselves are more conserved across all humans.

## Section 2.5 — Why Do New Cells Need to Be Produced?

**Q22.** Skin cells divide rapidly throughout life, while nerve cells in the cerebral cortex almost never divide after birth. Using the cell cycle logic, explain why this difference exists and its advantage to the organism.

**Q23.** Red blood cells are replaced every 120 days, yet approximately 20 million are destroyed every minute. If mitosis suddenly stopped in the bone marrow, trace the exact chain of events that would threaten oxygen transport in the body.

**Q24.** In a growing embryo, cells must divide while simultaneously specialising. How does the timing of mitosis relative to differentiation allow both growth and organisation to occur together?

**Q25.** Why is meiosis restricted to only the germinal cells in reproductive organs? What would be the long-term consequence for the species if body cells also underwent meiosis?

## Section 2.6 — Mitosis

**Q26.** Why do spindle fibres attach exclusively at the centromere and not anywhere else on the chromosome? What would be the consequence for genetic fidelity if attachment occurred randomly along the arms?

**Q27.** In mitosis, the nuclear membrane disappears completely in prophase and reappears in telophase. Why is this timed disappearance and reappearance mechanistically essential?

**Q28.** Visualise a plant cell in late anaphase. Why does it stretch longitudinally instead of pinching in the middle like an animal cell? Trace the physical forces involved.

**Q29.** Why must DNA replication be completed before the cell enters mitosis? What precise molecular mismatch would occur if even a small unreplicated segment remained?

**Q30.** Why does cytokinesis in animal cells use a contractile ring while plant cells build a cell plate from the centre outward? What fundamental difference in cell architecture forces these two different solutions?

**Q31.** What if the centromere on one chromosome failed to split during anaphase? Trace the chain of events that would follow in the two daughter cells.

**Q32.** Imagine a cell enters mitosis with unreplicated DNA (no sister chromatids). What mechanical disaster would occur when spindle fibres try to pull the chromosomes apart?

**Q33.** What would happen if a plant cell tried to use the animal-style furrowing method for cytokinesis? Why would this physically fail?

**Q34.** Compare the functional purpose of chromatin condensation in mitosis versus in meiosis (reduction division). Why does condensation alone not explain why one preserves chromosome number while the other halves it?

**Q35.** Contrast the significance of the centromere's role in mitosis with its role in meiosis I (homologous pair separation). How does the timing of centromere splitting differ between the two divisions and why?

**Q36.** Animal cells form asters and use furrowing; plant cells do neither. Which of these differences is merely cosmetic and which is a direct consequence of the presence/absence of a rigid cell wall? Justify.

**Q37.** *(Reverse Thinking)* A tissue sample shows many cells with chromosomes lined up at the equator but no further progress. Reverse-engineer the most likely defect and explain which stage has failed.

**Q38.** *(Reverse Thinking)* If a cell ends up with an extra chromosome in one daughter nucleus and one chromosome missing in the other, what single event during mitosis must have gone wrong? Trace it back to the exact stage.

**Q39.** *(Error Detection)* A student says: *"In mitosis, the chromosome number doubles so that each daughter cell gets more genetic material."* Spot the error and correct the understanding using replication versus division logic.

**Q40.** How does the difference in cytokinesis (furrow vs cell plate) relate to the presence of cell walls in plants and absence in animals? Extend this to explain why plant meristems are located only at growing tips and sides.

**Q41.** Predict the visible appearance under a microscope of a cell that has completed DNA replication but failed to condense its chromatin before prophase. What key feature would be missing?

**Q42.** If one sister chromatid accidentally loses its centromere protein entirely, predict the long-term genetic consequence for the cells descended from that division across multiple generations.

**Q43.** The textbook lists five significances of mitosis: growth, repair, replacement, asexual reproduction, and maintaining chromosome number. If the nuclear membrane failed to dissolve during prophase, which of these five would be most immediately and directly undermined? Justify step by step.

## Section 2.7 — Cell Cycle

**Q44.** Why is interphase called the "preparation phase" rather than the "resting phase"? Contrast what actually happens in G1, S and G2 with the external (microscopic) appearance of the nucleus during each sub-phase.

**Q45.** Suppose a cell is forced to undergo mitosis without first passing through G2 phase. What critical preparations would be missing, and what would be the most likely outcome for the daughter cells?

**Q46.** In late G1, every cell must choose one of two paths: enter S-phase and continue dividing, or withdraw into the resting phase (R). Using the specific examples from the chapter — nerve cells, liver cells, and skin cells — explain what shapes this decision and what the long-term consequence is for each cell type and for the organism.

**Q47.** Predict what would happen to tissue repair in an adult human if all cells permanently exited the cell cycle after G1 and entered the resting phase (R). Support with specific examples from skin, blood and liver.

**Q48.** If a chemical blocks DNA strand separation during S-phase entirely, at which subsequent stage of the cell cycle would mitosis halt, and why would the resulting daughter cells be non-viable?

**Q49.** *(Multi-step)* Visualise the entire cell cycle as a clock. If the G2 checkpoint is defective and cells rush into mitosis with incomplete DNA repair, construct the multi-step cascade that could lead to a tumour — linking chromosome structure, the stages of mitosis, and uncontrolled division.

## Section 2.8 — Meiosis

**Q50.** Compare the outcome of one mitotic division versus one complete meiotic division in terms of: (a) number of daughter cells, (b) chromosome number per cell, and (c) genetic identity. What single decision at the start of division creates all three differences?

**Q51.** *(Reverse Thinking)* You observe two daughter cells, each with exactly half the chromosome number of the parent cell. Working backwards, what must have happened inside the parent cell, and in which type of division did the critical event occur?

**Q52.** *(Error Detection)* Someone states: *"Meiosis produces four identical gametes."* Identify the two fundamental mistakes and correct them using crossing-over and independent assortment concepts.

**Q53.** Suppose crossing-over in meiosis fails completely in one parent. Step-by-step, explain how this would reduce genetic variation in offspring and link it back to the chromosome structure (chiasmata and chromatid exchange).

**Q54.** *(Multi-step)* Imagine a cell with 46 chromosomes. It completes one full mitotic division, then one of the daughter cells immediately undergoes meiosis. How many chromosomes will each of the final four cells have, and why does this sequence violate normal biology?

**Q55.** *(Multi-step)* A single fertilised egg divides mitotically to form a 16-cell embryo. Now suppose one of those 16 cells undergoes meiosis instead. Predict the chromosome content of the resulting cells and explain why this would be catastrophic for embryonic development.

## Cross-Chapter Integration ⚡

**Q56.** Connect the semi-conservative nature of DNA replication with the requirement for equal chromosome distribution in mitosis. What would happen to genetic fidelity over multiple generations if either mechanism were broken?

**Q57.** *(Highest Difficulty)* In a plant root tip, cells at the very tip divide rapidly while cells slightly further back differentiate and elongate. Using all three knowledge areas — cell cycle phases, chromosome packaging, and cytokinesis differences — reason why this spatial separation of division and differentiation is essential for proper root growth, and what would break down if cells tried to divide and differentiate simultaneously at the same location.
