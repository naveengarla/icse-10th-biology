# Chapter 2 — 20 Stress Test Questions 🔥

**Structure of Chromosomes, Cell Cycle and Cell Division**

> These 20 questions are engineered to **break shallow understanding**.  
> Each targets a specific conceptual weakness — not random facts.  
> Before answering, close your notes. Think. Then check [2.11_Answers](./2.11_Practice_Questions_Answers.md) or reason it out yourself.  
> Recommended use: **2 questions per day, go deep on each one.**

---

## Part 1 — DNA & Chromosome Understanding

**Q1.** DNA is roughly 2 metres long in every cell, yet the nucleus it lives in is about 6 micrometres across — that's a 10,000:1 compression. Explain *how* the cell achieves this packing AND *why* the packing state must change during cell division. What breaks if it doesn't change?

---

**Q2.** A student says: *"Chromosomes and chromatin are different structures."*

Do you agree or disagree? Explain in a way that removes this confusion completely — not just for the student, but for anyone who hears it.

---

**Q3.** Histone proteins are suddenly removed from a cell.  
Predict what happens to each of these — step by step:

- DNA structure inside the nucleus
- The cell's ability to divide
- The cell's ability to express genes

---

**Q4.** Why is DNA double-stranded rather than single-stranded?  
Don't give the definition — give a **practical advantage this creates during replication** that would be impossible with a single strand.

---

## Part 2 — DNA Replication (Deep Logic)

**Q5.** DNA replication happens *before* cell division (in S-phase), not after. Why is this sequence non-negotiable? Construct the exact problem that would occur if a cell tried to divide first and replicate later.

---

**Q6.** A mistake happens during DNA replication in S-phase — one nucleotide is incorrectly paired.  
Trace the consequence at each level:

- Immediately after the error in S-phase
- During mitosis
- In the organism over multiple generations

---

**Q7.** Why is strict base pairing (A pairs only with T, G pairs only with C) critical to the function of DNA?  
What specifically breaks if base pairing becomes random — covering both replication and gene function.

---

## Part 3 — Mitosis: Mechanism Stress Test

**Q8.** Why must every chromosome align *precisely* at the equatorial plate during metaphase — not slightly off to one side?  
What is the consequence if even one chromosome is mispositioned when anaphase begins?

---

**Q9.** Spindle fibres "pull" chromosomes to the poles. But *how* does a protein fibre generate movement?  
Explain the mechanism idea — not just that the fibre contracts, but what physical process makes the chromosome move toward the pole.

---

**Q10.** A cell successfully completes karyokinesis (nuclear division) but cytokinesis completely fails.  
What type of cell will result? What problems can this cause for the tissue and the organism?

---

**Q11.** The nuclear membrane dissolves during prophase and reforms during telophase.  
Explain mechanistically *why* it must disappear. Then explain what would go wrong if it stayed intact throughout mitosis.

---

## Part 4 — Error & Failure Scenarios

**Q12.** During anaphase, the sister chromatids of one chromosome fail to separate — both are pulled to the same pole.  
Describe precisely what the two daughter cells will look like in terms of chromosome number, and what the biological consequences are for each.

---

**Q13.** A mutation damages the centromere proteins on one chromosome so spindle fibres cannot attach.  
Which specific stage of mitosis is most disrupted? Step through exactly what happens from that stage onward.

---

**Q14.** A cell undergoes DNA replication **twice** in S-phase but only divides **once** in M-phase.  
Predict the chromosome condition (number and structure) in each daughter cell. Is this compatible with life?

---

## Part 5 — Cell Cycle Intelligence

**Q15.** The textbook used to call interphase the "resting phase." This is wrong.  
Explain what is *actually* happening in G1, S, and G2 to justify why "preparation phase" is far more accurate.

---

**Q16.** Nerve cells in the cerebral cortex stop dividing permanently after early childhood. Skin cells divide continuously throughout life.  
Using cell cycle logic, explain why this difference exists and why it is the correct strategy for each cell type — not a design flaw.

---

**Q17.** Uncontrolled, non-stop cell division leads to tumours.  
Which specific part of the cell cycle control has broken down? Trace how a single checkpoint failure can lead to a cascade ending in uncontrolled proliferation.

---

## Part 6 — Mitosis vs Meiosis (Core Reasoning)

**Q18.** Meiosis is essential for sexual reproduction. But why can't mitosis do the same job?  
Construct the exact arithmetic problem that would occur after a few generations if gametes were produced by mitosis rather than meiosis.

---

**Q19.** Meiosis produces genetic variation; mitosis does not. Why?  
Explain using the **actual mechanisms** — what physically happens in meiosis that doesn't happen in mitosis — not just the conclusion.

---

**Q20.** ⚡ *Elite Question*

Imagine a world where meiosis does not exist — all reproduction, including gamete formation, uses only mitosis.

Predict what happens over generations to:

- Genetic diversity within the population
- The species' ability to evolve
- Long-term survival of the species

Build your answer as a logical argument, not a list of facts.
