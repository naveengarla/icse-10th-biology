# Section 2.2: Discovery of Chromosomes

> *"Let us journey back to the year 1882. The modern laboratory does not yet exist. A German scientist, Walther Fleming, sits quietly in a dimly lit room, peering intently through an antiquated brass microscope at the rapidly growing larvae of a salamander..."*

## 🔬 The Man Who Saw the Threads (Walther Fleming)

Who actually discovered these "coloured bodies"? 

It was **Walther Fleming** in **1882**. While observing the rapidly dividing cells of an amphibian (the salamander), his old-school microscope revealed something extraordinary. He noticed tiny, delicate threads within the nucleus that appeared to be actively dividing straight down the middle, lengthwise. 

Because they looked so much like little moving threads, he gave this monumental process a name that would echo through history: **Mitosis**.
- **Mitos** literally means "thread" in Ancient Greek.

So, whenever you say "Mitosis" during an ICSE exam, you are essentially speaking Ancient Greek for *"The process of the threads"!* 

Subsequently, as centuries passed and our microscopes grew unimaginably powerful—leading up to the electron microscopes of today—these simple threads were revealed to be the astonishingly complex structures we now study.
