# Section 2.3: Structure of Chromosomes

*This is the biggest and most heavily-tested section in the chapter!*

## 🩻 1. The Anatomy of the "X"
During cell division, a condensed chromosome looks like an "X". 
- **Chromatids:** The two identical "arms" or "legs" of the chromosome. 
- **Centromere:** The tiny pinched region (constriction) that ties the two sister chromatids together. 

*Why is the centromere important?* It serves as the exact attachment point for **spindle fibres** during cell division! When the cell divides, the spindle fibers pull the two chromatids apart at this centromere.

## 🧬 2. What is Chromatin actually made of?
Chromatin material is exactly two things:
1. **DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid)** — Makes up about **40%**.
2. **Histones** — A specific type of protein making up about **60%**.

### The Packing Game: Nucleosomes
You have 2 meters of DNA in your cell! To prevent it from tangling, the DNA thread wraps around a core of **8 histone molecules** (like winding string around a football).
👉 **Exam Term:** This ball of 8 histones wrapped with DNA is called a **Nucleosome**.
*(Fun Fact: A single human chromosome can have about a million nucleosomes!)*

## 🪜 3. Structure of DNA (The Double Helix)
The shape of DNA was studied by **Rosalind Franklin (1953)** and fully figured out by **Watson and Crick**.

DNA is a macromolecule consisting of two complementary strands wound into a **Double Helix** (like a spiraling ladder). 
A single DNA strand is made of repeating building blocks called **Nucleotides**. 

Every nucleotide has **3 parts**:
1. A **Phosphate**
2. A **Sugar** (Pentose)
3. A **Nitrogenous Base**

### 🔥 The Rungs of the Ladder (The Bases)
There are 4 types of bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), and Thymine (T). 
**The Pairing Rule:**
- **A** pairs with **T** (with exactly **2 hydrogen bonds**).
- **G** pairs with **C** (with exactly **3 hydrogen bonds**).
*(Memory Trick: **A**pples in the **T**ree, **C**ars in the **G**arage!)*

## 🖨️ 4. Formation of New DNA (Replication)
During the *interphase* of the cell cycle, DNA duplicates itself.
The double helix unwinds and opens at one end. New strands perfectly form against each old strand. The result? Two identical double-helixes, where each has one "old" strand and one "new" strand.
